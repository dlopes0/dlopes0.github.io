Step 1 - SD Card

Normally, there are some kits that already include the Raspberry Pi, with the bonus of an SD card with a preinstalled operating system.

That card is often not included when you purchase the board. Being that so, you can buy it from any store or you can take it from other cellphone you may or may not own. For the new Raspberry Pi, you'll need a Micro SD card, the ones that are really small.

Step 2 - Raspberry Pi

What is a Raspberry Pi, you ask? A Raspberry Pi is a computer with the size of the palm of your hand that allows one to do magic. Essentially magic.

It has forty "doors" in total that can be controlled by coding them, opening and closing the passage for electricity With this, we can turn lights on and off. Make big wheels turn at any speed we want. Know how cold is outside. It can even determine the distance between two walls at home. Or maybe a robot that can dodge obstacles.

There are various Raspberry Pi models:

Raspberry Pi (2012-2014)

Being the oldest model (the first, actually!), it doesn't have access to WiFi, it has a rather slow processor and less memory (between 256MB and 512MB).

It has also, in opposite to the other Raspberry Pies, twenty-six pins - those controllable "doors" that I talked about before.

It has been discontinued, being very hard to find and get one.

Raspberry Pi 2 (2015)

It has a superior processor compared to the previous computer, and it has a lot more memory (1GB, two times 512MB or four times 256MB!).

Just like the modern Raspberry Pi, it has forty pins, unlike the previous one.

Raspberry Pi Zero (2015)

It's an ultra small version of the first Raspberry Pi. And just like the original, it has the same processor, the same memory size, but this one has forty pins.

But just like the first one, it can't connect to WiFi.

Unfortunately, being very small, it hasn't got an Ethernet port to connect to the Home Router or an Ethernet Wall Socket.

Raspberry Pi 3 (2016-2018)

It has three versions with the same improved processor in comparison to the previous - B, A+ and B+.

The oldest version is the "B" one. It has over a 1GB of memory, Ethernet port, WiFi support, and forty pins.

The most compact version is the "A" one. It has 512GB of memory, lacks an Ethernet port, but it has WiFi support and forty pins. It's a way smaller and compact version of the model "B".

The most recent and improved is the model "B+". It has 1GB of memory, Gigabit Ethernet port (way faster Ethernet), WiFi support and forty pins.

Raspberry Pi Zero W (2017)

It's an updated Raspberry Pi Zero version, but with WiFi support and keeping its small size.

Raspberry Pi 4 (2019-2020)

With a way faster processor compared to the other Raspberry Pies, more memory (from 1GB to 8GB!), Gigabit Ethernet and WiFi support! The specs are great!

It is expected from a modern computer.

Raspberry Pi 400 (2020)

It is a version of the Raspberry Pi 4 (4GB) inside a keyboard, reassembling the looks of an 80s retro computer.

Extremely useful for someone that doesn't want to "steal" the home computers' keyboard, those that haven't a spare one.

Raspberry Pi Zero 2W (2021)

Another updated version of the Raspberry Pi Zero, but with a far better processor plus WiFi support.

You can find out more, and know where to buy at the Raspberry Pi official website.

Step 3 - Putting the Operating System into the Card

To put the operating system we just bought, we must insert it on our computer or laptop through an SD port, using any Micro SD to SD adapter, that is often included when you buy the Micro SD card. In case the computer doesn't have an SD port, a Micro SD to USB adapter is required. These adapters are very easy to come by, since you can find them in any store that normally sell acessories for digital or video cameras.

As soon as the card is correctly inserted on the computer, you will need to use an official Raspberry Pi tool called "Imager", that allows the Operating System to be written on the card.

It can be downloaded through the official site at this link.

Then, you enter the application and through the menus, you select the recommended operating system, as well as selecting the SD Card that we're going to use, and finally click "WRITE".

Any problem you encounter can be solved by watching this small official setup video.

Step 4 - Connecting everything

To start connecting everything, you must follow these steps:

  1. Connect the HDMI cable from the TV or monitor to the Raspberry. On Raspberry Pi 4 and 400, the HDMI cable has a regular plug at one end, and a smaller funnier plug at the other. It's called a Micro HDMI-HDMI cable. Google it.
  2. Insert the SD card with the operating system at the Micro SD card that you can find at the bottom of the board
  3. (optional) Connect the Ethernet cable to the router, switch, powerline, hub or Ethernet port in case you can't connect to any WiFi network
  4. Connect a USB Mouse to a USB port
  5. (optional for Raspberry 400) Connect a USB Keyboard to a USB port
  6. Connect the official power supply to the Raspberry Pi, or a regular phone charger (not recommended)
  7. It is already on! The Raspberry Pi does not have an on/off button!

Step 2 - Circuit Assembly

Assemble the circuit just like in the following image:

⚠ Warning! The size of the LED legs do matter! The larger leg is connected to the resistor and the smaller leg is connected to the black cable.

Just like the batteries that you use for toys and such, the LEDs have positive and negative sides. The positive side corresponds to the largest leg, and the negative side corresponds to the shortest leg. If we invert the LED, we may ruin it for good.

What happens in the circuit is simple - the electricity leaves the board through the pin, going through the cable and breadboard until finding the resistor. The resistor makes sure that the electrons that make the electricity (a sort of funny, but annoying particles that can't stop moving) go through it at a great speed, just like the Stop sign at a road. After they become slower, these electrons pass through the LED without ruining it.

If they had been passing quicker, they would have destroyed and burned the LED, given that inside it there's a thin cable, and not every electron can pass at the same time. Because when they run at a great speed, that cable begins to melt, because they start getting much less space to run.

When they get through the LED without ruining it, the LED turns on, because the electrons are running safely and passing through the LED at a lower rate.

At the end, the electrons go back home at the board through the ground pin.

In this circuit, the ground pin is the most important, because without it, the electrons wouldn't want to run through our circuit.

Before getting into the code, we must understand how a breadboard works.

A breadboard is a board made out of plastic, that has thin metal strips inside it. These strips are used to conduct electricity.

At a closer look, the breadboard has two columns of holes in each side. Each column has a vertical metal strip below it.

It has also numbers on the interior, that represent rows. Each number represent a number. Each row has metal strip below it.

With these four metal columns and remaining rows, it is possible to build a circuit without needing lots of cables.
